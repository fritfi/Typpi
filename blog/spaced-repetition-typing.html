Spaced Repetition and Typing: A Powerful Duo

How to hack your brain's forgetting curve using simple tools.

We've all been there: you spend hours cramming for an exam, feel confident, and then a week later, the information has vanished. This is due to the "Forgetting Curve," a concept discovered by Hermann Ebbinghaus. But there's a solution: Spaced Repetition.

What is Spaced Repetition?

Spaced repetition is a learning technique where you review material at increasing intervals. Instead of reviewing your notes five times in one day, you review them once today, once tomorrow, once in three days, and once next week.

This forces your brain to work harder to retrieve the information just as it's about to be forgotten, which strengthens the memory trace.

Adding Active Typing to the Mix

While spaced repetition is usually associated with flashcards (like Anki), combining it with active typing can be even more effective for understanding complex concepts.

Flashcards are great for isolated facts, but typing out full paragraphs or definitions forces you to engage with the structure and context of the information.

How to Implement This Strategy

  1. Day 1: Type out your lecture notes or summary in Typpi. Focus on accuracy and understanding.
  2. Day 2: Re-type the most difficult sections. If you make mistakes, slow down.
  3. Day 4: Try to type the key concepts from memory first, then check your source text and type it out in Typpi to correct yourself.
  4. Day 7: Do a final "speed run" of the material to ensure fluency.

Why It Works

By re-typing the material at spaced intervals, you are combining motor memory (from typing) with cognitive recall (from spaced repetition). This dual-coding approach creates multiple pathways in your brain to access the same information.

Conclusion

Don't just read your notes over and over. Type them. Space them out. Challenge your brain. You'll be surprised at how much more you remember with less total study time.
